Once upon a time, in the heart of Paris...

9:30 AM - The Morning

It was a Saturday morning like any other at the Louvre Museum. Tourists shuffled through the grand halls, cameras clicking at priceless masterpieces. But in the Apollo Gallery, where France's crown jewels glittered under museum lights, something extraordinary was about to unfold. The most audacious heist in modern French history was beginning—and it would all be over in seven minutes.

Outside the museum, a moving truck with an extendable furniture lift pulled up alongside the building. Four men, dressed in high-visibility vests and construction helmets, moved with purpose. To anyone watching, they looked like maintenance workers. But these were no ordinary workers. They were thieves, and they knew exactly where they were going.

The Entry

The mechanical cherry picker extended upward to the second-floor balcony of the Apollo Gallery. The men climbed up, power tools in hand. With a circular saw and an angle grinder, they cut through the window and burst inside.

A Louvre security guard saw them and began filming, but the thieves moved with terrifying efficiency. They weren't there to steal everything—they were after specific pieces. Their planning was meticulous. Their execution was flawless. And they had only minutes to complete their mission.

The Theft

Using their power tools, the masked thieves shattered two high-security display cases. They grabbed eight pieces of France's crown jewels—Napoleon's emerald necklace, a diamond and sapphire tiara worn by queens, jeweled sets worth an estimated $102 million.

They tried to take Empress Eugénie's ornate gold crown studded with 1,354 diamonds and 56 emeralds, but in their haste, they dropped it. The crown would later be found damaged outside the museum—a costly mistake that would become a crucial piece of evidence.

The Escape

In their chaotic rush to escape, the thieves left behind a trail of evidence: a motorcycle helmet, gloves, a walkie-talkie, a gas can, and their reflective vests. But most importantly, they left behind something invisible to the naked eye—their DNA.

They scrambled back down the cherry picker and sped away on two waiting motorcycles, weaving through Paris traffic. The entire operation—from entry to escape—took less than ten minutes. By the time police arrived, they were gone. The heist was complete. Or so they thought.

The Manhunt

What followed was one of the largest manhunts in recent French history. Over 100 investigators worked around the clock, analyzing more than 150 DNA samples and examining the equipment left at the scene.

Security footage was reviewed. Phone records were traced. DNA databases were searched. And slowly, the net began to close. One by one, the suspects were identified. Some were arrested at their homes. One was caught at Charles de Gaulle Airport trying to board a plane to Algeria. The heist that shocked France was beginning to unravel.

THE SUSPECTS

The faces behind France's most audacious heist

34-Year-Old Former Garbage Collector

Arrested: Oct. 25, while trying to board a plane for Algeria at Paris-Charles de Gaulle Airport.

His DNA was recovered from a scooter used in the getaway. This critical evidence linked him directly to the escape vehicles.

ARRESTED
39-Year-Old Taxi Driver

Arrested: Oct. 25 at his home in the Paris suburb of Aubervilliers.

His DNA was found on a window at the Louvre. He was previously convicted in an aggravated robbery. Partially admitted involvement under questioning.

ARRESTED
37-Year-Old Man

Arrested: Oct. 29 in the Paris suburb of Seine-Saint-Denis.

His DNA was found on the mechanical lift used to break into the museum. He has 11 previous convictions, 10 for theft. Denied involvement in the heist.

ARRESTED
38-Year-Old Woman

Arrested: Oct. 29 in Seine-Saint-Denis.

She is the longtime partner of the 37-year-old suspect and has denied involvement in the heist. Charged as an accomplice in organized robbery and criminal conspiracy.
